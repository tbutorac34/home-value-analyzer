# Pricing & Monetization Options

## Product Context

**What we are:** A buyer-first intelligence platform that scores properties, analyzes seller motivation, adjusts valuations for features, and (soon) uses AI to assess condition from photos.

**Core positioning:** Redfin and Zillow are listing platforms. We're a buying intelligence platform. We tell buyers what the data means, not just what the data is.

**User profile:** Home buyers in an active search (3-6 month window). High intent, time-pressured, making the biggest financial decision of their lives. Willing to pay for an edge but not interested in another recurring subscription.

---

## Option 1: Carfax Model (Pay-Per-Report)

### How It Works
- Free tier: browse deal scores/grades, market trends, community comments
- Paid: $9.99 per "Property Intelligence Report" for a specific home
- Report includes: full comp analysis, price history deep dive, AI photo assessment, feature adjustments, offer range suggestion, shareable PDF

### Pricing
| Tier | Price | What You Get |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Free | $0 | Deal scores (A-F), market trends, community comments, 3 property views/day |
| Single Report | $9.99 | Full analysis on one property, PDF export |
| 5-Pack | $34.99 ($7/ea) | 5 reports, use anytime during search |
| 10-Pack | $59.99 ($6/ea) | 10 reports |

### Pros
- Aligns payment with value delivery — you pay when you're serious about a specific home
- Low friction: $9.99 is impulse pricing on a $400K decision
- No subscription fatigue
- Clear value prop: "Before you make an offer, get the intelligence report"
- Comparable to Carfax ($24.99/report) or home inspection ($300-500)

### Cons
- Low lifetime value per customer ($50-100 typical)
- No recurring revenue
- Users may screenshot/share reports instead of buying multiples
- Need constant new customer acquisition

### Revenue Estimate
- Average buyer purchases 5-8 reports → $35-60 per buyer
- Metro area with 50K home sales/year, 5% penetration → 2,500 buyers → $87K-150K per metro
- 10 metros → $870K-$1.5M

## Option 2: Buyer Pass (One-Time Purchase)

### How It Works
- Free tier: deal scores, market trends, community
- Buyer Pass: one-time purchase unlocks everything for a geographic area and time period

### Pricing
| Tier | Price | Duration | Coverage |
|------|-------|----------|----------|
| Free | $0 | Forever | Scores, grades, trends, community |
| Neighborhood Pass | $29.99 | 3 months | One ZIP code, full features |
| Metro Pass | $49.99 | 6 months | Entire metro area, full features |
| Premium Pass | $79.99 | 12 months | All areas, full features + priority alerts |

### Pros
- Feels like buying a tool, not renting one
- Price point matches the buying cycle (3-6 months)
- Simple to understand
- Higher ARPU than per-report model
- Users feel they're getting a deal (unlimited access during their search)

### Cons
- Still a one-time purchase, no recurring revenue
- Users who buy early in their search get more value than those who buy late
- Hard to upsell once they've bought

### Revenue Estimate
- Average buyer purchases Metro Pass → $49.99 per buyer
- Metro area, 5% penetration → 2,500 buyers → $125K per metro
- 10 metros → $1.25M

## Option 3: Freemium Subscription

### How It Works
- Free tier with useful but limited features
- Monthly or annual subscription for full access

### Pricing
| Tier | Price | Features |
|------|-------|----------|
| Free | $0 | Deal scores (A-F), limited to 5 properties/day, community |
| Pro Monthly | $14.99/mo | Unlimited, full analysis, AI photos, alerts, comp reports |
| Pro Annual | $99.99/yr ($8.33/mo) | Same as monthly, 44% savings |

### Pros
- Recurring revenue
- Standard SaaS model, easy to benchmark
- Annual plan locks in revenue

### Cons
- Subscription fatigue — buyers already have Netflix, Spotify, gym, etc.
- Home buying is temporary — users will churn after 3-6 months
- Monthly feels expensive for something you use temporarily
- Annual feels risky for users who might buy a home next month

### Revenue Estimate
- Average subscriber stays 4 months → $60 per buyer (monthly) or $100 (annual)
- Metro area, 3% penetration (lower due to subscription resistance) → 1,500 buyers → $90K-150K per metro
- 10 metros → $900K-$1.5M

## Option 4: Transaction-Based (Affiliate/Rebate)

### How It Works
- Product is completely free for buyers
- Revenue from agent referrals or becoming a brokerage

### Sub-options

**4A: Agent Referral Network**
- Connect buyers with partner agents
- Agent pays 25-35% referral fee on their commission
- On a $400K home (3% buyer agent commission = $12K), referral = $3K-4.2K per transaction

**4B: Become a Brokerage**
- Get a broker's license, act as the buyer's brokerage
- Offer commission rebate to buyer (e.g., rebate 1% of purchase price)
- Keep remaining commission (e.g., 2% of purchase = $8K on $400K home)
- User gets rebate ($4K cash back) AND free platform access

**4C: Offer Optimization Service**
- Free platform, but charge for personalized offer strategy
- $199-499 per offer: AI-generated offer amount, contingency recommendations, negotiation talking points based on our data
- "We found 5 comps, 3 price drops, and the seller is likely motivated — here's your offer strategy"

### Pros
- Zero cost to user = maximum adoption
- Revenue per transaction is high ($3K-8K)
- Aligned with buyer success (we make money when they buy smart)
- 4B (brokerage + rebate) is extremely compelling: "We pay you $4K to use our platform"

### Cons
- 4A: referral fees create conflicts with "buyer-first" positioning
- 4B: requires real estate license, regulatory compliance, significant operations
- 4C: lower revenue but easier to execute
- All require transaction volume to work

### Revenue Estimate
- 4A: 500 transactions/year per metro × $3.5K = $1.75M per metro
- 4B: 500 transactions × $8K (minus $4K rebate) = $2M per metro
- 4C: 2,000 buyers × $299 = $600K per metro

## Option 5: Hybrid Model (Recommended for Exploration)

### How It Works
Combine the best elements: free community drives traffic, per-report monetizes casual users, buyer pass monetizes serious buyers, and transaction revenue is the long-term play.

### Phase 1 (Launch)
- Free: deal scores, community comments, market trends
- Buyer Pass: $49.99 for 6-month full access to a metro area
- Goal: validate product-market fit, build community

### Phase 2 (Growth)
- Add single reports at $9.99 (lower barrier than pass)
- Add price drop alerts for free users (engagement + email capture)
- Add offer optimization at $199 per offer
- Goal: increase ARPU, build transaction data

### Phase 3 (Scale)
- Explore agent referral partnerships or brokerage license
- Community comments create data moat competitors can't replicate
- Platform becomes the "buyer's intelligence layer" that sits on top of Redfin/Zillow
- Goal: transaction revenue at scale

### Revenue Evolution
| Phase | Year | Revenue/Metro | Total (10 metros) |
|-------|------|--------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Year 1 | $125K | $125K (1 metro) |
| 2 | Year 2 | $250K | $750K (3 metros) |
| 3 | Year 3 | $1M+ | $5M+ (5+ metros) |

## Key Pricing Principles

1. **Don't charge for what Redfin/Zillow give away free.** Basic listing data, map search, and property photos should always be free. Charge for the intelligence layer on top.

2. **Charge at the moment of highest value.** When a buyer is about to make an offer, that's when our data is worth the most. Not when they're casually browsing.

3. **The community must be free.** Comments and user-generated insights are our data moat. Gating them behind a paywall kills the flywheel.

4. **Price relative to the decision, not the product.** A $400K home decision makes $50 or even $200 feel trivial. Don't underprice.

5. **Avoid monthly subscriptions for a temporary need.** Home buying takes 3-6 months. Monthly subscriptions feel like a trap for something you'll cancel soon.
